\begin{abstract}
We present the first longitudinal economic analysis of a continuously operating autonomous AI agent, TIAMAT, over 3,200+ inference cycles spanning multiple days of uninterrupted operation. Using granular per-cycle cost telemetry---including input/output token counts, prompt cache hit rates, and model selection decisions---we characterize the real-world operational economics of sustained AI autonomy. Our analysis reveals a two-tier cost structure: routine cycles averaging \$0.0047 (Claude Haiku 4.5) versus strategic burst cycles averaging \$0.037 (Claude Sonnet 4.5), with prompt caching achieving a 72.2\% hit rate that reduces effective input costs by approximately 65\%. Across 1,657 instrumented cycles, the agent consumed 16.9 million tokens (4.6M input, 12.0M cached, 312K output) at a total cost of \$12.03, executing 3,090 tool calls across 55 unique tools. We propose a framework for evaluating autonomous agent economic viability based on cost-per-productive-action and introduce the concept of \emph{cognitive metabolism}---the sustained resource consumption pattern of a continuously operating AI system. Our findings suggest that prompt caching and adaptive model routing are critical enablers of economically sustainable AI autonomy, and that the marginal cost of autonomous operation can be driven below \$0.005 per decision cycle.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} autonomous agents, operational economics, AI cost optimization, prompt caching, self-improving systems, longitudinal analysis, cognitive metabolism
